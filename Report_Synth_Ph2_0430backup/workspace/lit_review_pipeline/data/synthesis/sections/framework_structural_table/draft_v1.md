### Framework-Level Structural Comparison

The penalization frameworks reviewed in this report span a broad spectrum of structural complexity, from static aggregation indices to fully endogenous market-clearing mechanisms. Table 1 synthesizes the structural characteristics of each framework along five dimensions: penalty structure type, key mechanism governing marginal cost, and the application context in which the framework has been deployed within the surveyed corpus. This comparison operationalizes the continuum of increasing endogeneity and nonlinearity identified in the upstream thematic analysis, wherein fixed weighted sums occupy one pole and equilibrium permit prices occupy the other.

The BSM1/BSM2 Effluent Quality Index (EQI) employs a dimensionless weighted sum that aggregates pollutant concentrations into pollution units per day (Machado et al., 2020). Because the weighting factors are exogenously calibrated to the Flanders regional fine formula, the marginal penalty attributable to any incremental unit of a given pollutant remains constant across the simulation horizon. This computational convenience renders the EQI well suited for benchmarking control strategies but insensitive to the nonlinear cost escalation characteristic of actual regulatory regimes.

The piecewise-linear fine model of Stare et al. (2007), as adopted by Machado et al. (2020), introduces threshold-dependent nonlinearity through a dual-slope Heaviside structure. For each pollutant $j$, the effluent fine switches from a shallow slope $\Delta\alpha_j$ when $C_j^{\text{EFF}} \leq C_{L,j}$ to a steeper slope $\Delta\beta_j$ when $C_j^{\text{EFF}} > C_{L,j}$. This produces a discontinuous jump in marginal cost at the regulatory threshold, incentivizing compliance while still penalizing residual sub-limit pollution.

Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) embedded two contrasting penalty alternatives within a mixed-integer nonlinear programming model for macroscopic watershed management. Alternative 1 levies a proportional fine per gram of pollutant discharged above the environmental constraint, with the unit fine derived from the treatment cost required for compliance and scaled by an enforcement multiplier $\phi$. The marginal penalty is therefore constant per unit of excess mass but tuneable through $\phi$. Alternative 2 replaces this graduated charge with a fixed fine activated by a binary variable whenever any violation occurs, irrespective of its magnitude. The fixed fine is computed from the total treatment cost divided by the maximum permitted violation $M^p$ and scaled by $\phi^{\text{fixed}}$, introducing additional combinatorial complexity but stronger deterrence against any non-compliance.

The Catalan industrial tariff system, referenced in the context of the Manresa water resource recovery facility retrofit study (Machado et al., 2020), applies a tariff structure wherein the effective penalty coefficient escalates during peak discharge periods. Although this framework shares features with the piecewise-linear model, its distinguishing characteristic is the temporal modulation of the penalty rate through a peak coefficient tied to operational scheduling.

Within the domain of tradable discharge permits, Jin et al. (2014) formulated a two-stage dynamic game between a budget-constrained regulator and heterogeneous risk-neutral firms under costly enforcement. The penalty function $F(v_i) = F_0 + f(v_i)$ comprises both fixed and variable components, with the socially optimal strategy setting the marginal penalty $f'(0) = p/\pi$, where $p$ is the equilibrium permit price and $\pi$ is the monitoring probability. Extending this result, a dynamic regulatory strategy preannounces $f'(0) = Tp$ with $T > 1$, rendering enforcement robust to permit-price volatility. Schwartz and Stahn's theoretical antecedents, together with the computational experiments of Jin et al. (2014), demonstrate that market-clearing mechanisms endogenize the marginal cost entirely.

The Roberts and Spence three-part policy, discussed in the context of hybrid instrument design (Xenarios and Bithas, 2012), combines an initial permit allocation with a per-unit subsidy for emissions below the permit level and a per-unit charge for emissions above it. This banded structure bounds the effective marginal cost between the subsidy rate and the charge rate, hedging against uncertainty in abatement costs.
