## Introduction and Scope

The activated sludge process in municipal wastewater treatment consumes substantial electrical energy, with aeration alone accounting for approximately 45 to 60% of total facility electricity use (Zohrabian et al., 2021). This energy intensity, combined with the growing need for flexible demand-side resources to support electric grids with high penetrations of variable renewable generation, has motivated research into leveraging wastewater treatment plants as participants in demand response (DR) programs (Zohrabian et al., 2021). Yet a fundamental translational problem persists: the simulation platforms most widely used to evaluate aeration control strategies—chief among them the Benchmark Simulation Model No. 1 (BSM1)—produce performance metrics that are standardized but not directly monetary. Converting these metrics into economically meaningful penalty signals that can inform DR scheduling decisions remains an under-addressed challenge.

BSM1 and its extensions employ two aggregate indices to assess plant performance: the Effluent Quality Index (EQI), which weights effluent concentrations of total suspended solids, chemical oxygen demand, biochemical oxygen demand, total Kjeldahl nitrogen, and nitrate into a single mass-based score, and the Operational Cost Index (OCI), which aggregates aeration energy, pumping energy, sludge production, and other operating expenditures into a dimensionless daily cost surrogate (Gernaey et al., 2014). Although both indices facilitate reproducible comparison of control strategies across research groups, neither directly yields a monetary fine or revenue figure. The EQI weighting factors originate from regulatory traditions—most notably the European Community Urban Waste Water Treatment Directive (EEC Council, 1991)—that impose concentration ceilings on pollutant discharges rather than continuous price schedules. Consequently, an EQI value alone cannot answer the question a utility DR manager must pose: "What financial penalty do I incur if I curtail aeration for two hours during a peak electricity price window?"

Several research groups have addressed this gap by appending monetary fine functions to BSM-type simulation outputs. Stare et al. (2007) introduced a piecewise-linear effluent fine formulation in which each pollutant $j$ incurs a cost that increases at a mild slope $\Delta\alpha_j$ when the effluent concentration $C_j^{\text{EFF}}$ remains below the discharge limit $C_{L,j}$, and at a steeper slope $\Delta\beta_j$ once the limit is exceeded—activated by a Heaviside step function. This two-slope structure has been adopted and extended in subsequent studies (Machado et al., 2020; Vanrolleghem and Gillot, 2002), producing daily effluent fines $EF$ in euros that enter a total operating cost expression alongside aeration energy costs at a conversion factor $\gamma_E = 0.1$ €/kWh and sludge treatment costs at $\gamma_{SP} = 0.5$ €/kg (Machado et al., 2020). In parallel, Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) formulated two alternative penalization architectures at the watershed scale: a proportional fine per unit mass of pollutant discharged above the environmental constraint and a fixed fine triggered by any degree of regulatory violation, with the magnitude of the fine parameter $\phi$ shown through sensitivity analysis to govern the economic–environmental trade-off decisively.

The tension that motivates an equilibrium framing arises because aeration flexibility and effluent quality are coupled through dissolved oxygen dynamics. Reducing or interrupting aeration to shed electrical load during peak price periods can degrade nitrification performance, elevate ammonia concentrations, and increase effluent turbidity (Zohrabian et al., 2021; Aghajanzadeh et al., 2015). Pilot studies have confirmed that while some facilities tolerate short aeration interruptions without measurable quality deterioration—particularly those with supplementary oxygenation sources—others experience immediate ammonia breakthroughs that would trigger regulatory fines (Zohrabian et al., 2021). The economic question therefore becomes one of balancing the marginal energy cost savings from DR participation against the marginal increase in effluent penalty, a structure that naturally lends itself to equilibrium or optimization formulations where the first-order conditions equate marginal benefits and marginal costs.

This review delineates four intersecting literatures that bear on this translational problem:

1. **Regulatory origins of EQI weighting factors and discharge limits.** The pollutant-specific weights embedded in the EQI derive from European and North American regulatory frameworks that set concentration ceilings rather than continuous damage functions (EEC Council, 1991). Understanding these origins clarifies why a direct EQI-to-fine conversion is not straightforward and why researchers have resorted to surrogate fine functions.

2. **Monetary fine functions applied to BSM outputs.** The family of piecewise-linear, Heaviside-switched, and fixed-fine formulations developed by Stare et al. (2007), Machado et al. (2020), and Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) constitute the primary mechanisms by which simulation-derived effluent concentrations are translated into cost terms amenable to optimization.

3. **Demand response literature on aeration flexibility.** Research on load shedding, load shifting, and intermittent aeration in wastewater treatment plants—reviewed comprehensively by Zohrabian et al. (2021)—establishes the operational constraints, quality risks, and energy savings magnitudes that any penalty-aware DR strategy must accommodate.

4. **Economic equilibrium frameworks bridging simulation to optimization.** Multi-objective and penalty-based optimization formulations, including the $\epsilon$-constraint approaches and sensitivity analyses of fine parameters explored by Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017), provide the mathematical scaffolding for identifying cost-minimizing operating points that respect both energy market signals and effluent quality constraints.

The scope of this review deliberately excludes two adjacent domains. Detailed activated sludge biokinetics—the ASM1 through ASM3 model families and their calibration—are treated as given inputs to the simulation environment rather than objects of review. Likewise, pure power-system DR economics that do not engage with effluent quality consequences fall outside the boundary; the review concentrates on frameworks where water quality penalties and energy costs are jointly considered. By synthesizing across these four literatures, the review aims to clarify how the research community has constructed—and might further refine—the economic bridge between dynamic aeration simulation and DR decision-making.

## Penalization Frameworks for Effluent Discharge

The regulation of wastewater effluent quality has given rise to a spectrum of penalization frameworks, ranging from simple weighted indices embedded in simulation benchmarks to endogenous market-clearing permit prices. This section synthesizes the principal families of frameworks encountered in the literature, organizing them along a complexity hierarchy that subsequent sections can exploit when comparing marginal-cost structures and optimization behavior.

### Dimensionless Weighted Pollution Units: The BSM1/BSM2 Approach

The Benchmark Simulation Models BSM1 and BSM2 employ a dimensionless effluent quality index that aggregates multiple pollutant concentrations into a single weighted sum expressed in pollution units per day. The weighting factors are calibrated to the Flanders regional fine formula, which assigns relative monetary weights to total suspended solids, chemical oxygen demand, biological oxygen demand, total Kjeldahl nitrogen, and nitrate-nitrogen. Because the weights are fixed and the aggregation is linear, the marginal cost attributed to any incremental gram of a given pollutant is constant within a simulation period. This renders the BSM framework computationally convenient for benchmarking control strategies but insensitive to the nonlinear cost escalation that real regulatory regimes impose once discharge limits are breached.

### Piecewise-Linear Monetary Fines: The Stare et al. Model

Stare et al. (2007) introduced a piecewise-linear effluent fine model that explicitly distinguishes between sub-limit and supra-limit discharge concentrations. For each pollutant $j$, the daily fine is computed as

$$EF_j = \Delta\alpha_j \, C_j^{\text{EFF}} + (\Delta\beta_j - \Delta\alpha_j)\,(C_j^{\text{EFF}} - C_{L,j})\,H(C_j^{\text{EFF}} - C_{L,j})$$

where $\Delta\alpha_j$ is the slope below the discharge limit $C_{L,j}$, $\Delta\beta_j$ is the steeper slope above that limit, and $H(\cdot)$ denotes the Heaviside step function (Machado et al., 2020; Stare et al., 2007). This dual-slope Heaviside structure produces a discontinuous jump in marginal cost at the regulatory threshold, creating a strong economic incentive for the plant to remain compliant while still penalizing residual pollution below the limit. The model has been deployed extensively in water resource recovery facility optimization studies, including retrofitting comparisons where effluent fines for ammonium, total nitrogen, and phosphate are summed alongside energy and sludge costs (Machado et al., 2020).

### Proportional and Fixed Alternatives: Garibay-Rodriguez et al.

Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) formulated two contrasting penalty alternatives within a mixed-integer nonlinear programming (MINLP) model for macroscopic watershed management. Under Alternative 1, a proportional fine $F^d$ (or $F^i$ for industrial sources) is charged per gram of pollutant discharged above the environmental constraint. The fine magnitude is derived from the unit treatment cost required to achieve compliance, scaled by an enforcement multiplier $\phi$ such that $F^d = \phi \cdot CPG^d$, where $CPG^d$ is the cost per gram of pollution reduction through technology alone (Garibay-Rodriguez et al., 2017). This proportional structure yields a marginal penalty that is constant per unit of excess mass but tuneable through $\phi$; sensitivity analysis demonstrates that the system's economic–environmental trade-off is highly responsive to $\phi$, with values near 0.1 representing an inflection point beyond which additional penalty increases raise costs without further reducing discharge concentrations (Garibay-Rodriguez et al., 2017).

Alternative 2 replaces the proportional charge with a fixed fine $CFF^d$ activated by a binary variable $w^{p}_{r,d,l,t}$ whenever the discharge limit is violated, irrespective of the magnitude of transgression. The fixed fine is derived from the total treatment cost divided by the maximum permitted violation $M^p$ and scaled by $\phi^{\text{fixed}}$. Although the fixed-fine alternative adds combinatorial complexity through additional binary variables, it offers stronger environmental protection in the studied cases, since it discourages any violation rather than merely pricing marginal exceedances (Garibay-Rodriguez et al., 2017).

### The Catalonia Industrial Tariff

Regional tariff systems, such as the Catalonia industrial wastewater tariff, incorporate peak-flow coefficients and volume saturation mechanisms that produce nonlinear effective penalties. A peak coefficient multiplies the base tariff rate during periods of hydraulic overload, while a saturation function caps the volumetric charge at high discharge rates, reflecting diminishing marginal infrastructure cost beyond design capacity. These features embed an implicit penalty for both concentration and flow exceedances within the tariff structure itself, rather than through an external fine.

### Equilibrium Permit Market Frameworks

At the most complex end of the hierarchy lie frameworks in which the marginal cost of pollution is determined endogenously through market equilibrium rather than being set administratively. Schwartz and Stahn developed theoretical conditions under which tradable discharge permits achieve cost-effective pollution control in water systems, leveraging the allocative efficiency of price-clearing markets. Jin et al. (2014) extended this line of inquiry by studying a two-stage dynamic game between a regulator and heterogeneous risk-neutral firms under costly enforcement. Their model specifies a penalty function $F(v_i) = F_0 + f(v_i)$ with both fixed ($F_0$) and variable components, and demonstrates that the socially optimal regulatory strategy is to induce full compliance by setting the marginal penalty equal to the permit price divided by the monitoring probability, i.e., $f'(0) = p/\pi$ (Jin et al., 2014). A key insight is that integrating the permit market price directly into the penalty shape — so that $f'(0) = Tp$ with $T > 1$ — endogenizes the penalty and renders the enforcement strategy robust to permit-price volatility.

Roberts and Spence proposed a three-part policy combining an initial permit allocation, a per-unit subsidy for emissions below the permit level, and a per-unit charge for emissions above it. This hybrid instrument bounds the effective marginal cost between the subsidy rate and the charge rate, hedging against the regulator's uncertainty about abatement costs. Xenarios and Bithas (2012) corroborate that hybrid instruments combining standards with taxes or permits frequently outperform any single tool, a finding consistent with the broader environmental economics literature on second-best instrument choice.

### Complexity Hierarchy

The frameworks described above can be ordered along a continuum of increasing endogeneity and nonlinearity. At one extreme, the BSM weighted sum assigns fixed, exogenous weights that yield constant marginal penalties. The Stare et al. piecewise model introduces a threshold nonlinearity through the Heaviside function, producing two distinct marginal-cost regimes. The Garibay-Rodriguez proportional and fixed alternatives embed the penalty within an optimization model, making the realized penalty a decision variable modulated by $\phi$ or by binary activation. The Catalonia tariff further complicates matters with volume-dependent saturation. Finally, the permit-market frameworks of Jin et al. (2014) and the Roberts–Spence hybrid render the marginal cost fully endogenous, determined by market clearing and regulatory design simultaneously. This hierarchy — from fixed weighted sums through piecewise functions to endogenous equilibrium prices — provides the organizational scaffold for the comparative analyses that follow.

The penalization frameworks reviewed in this report span a broad spectrum of structural complexity, from static aggregation indices to fully endogenous market-clearing mechanisms. This comparison operationalizes the continuum of increasing endogeneity and nonlinearity identified in the upstream thematic analysis, wherein fixed weighted sums occupy one pole and equilibrium permit prices occupy the other.

The BSM1/BSM2 Effluent Quality Index (EQI) employs a dimensionless weighted sum that aggregates pollutant concentrations into pollution units per day (Machado et al., 2020). Because the weighting factors are exogenously calibrated to the Flanders regional fine formula, the marginal penalty attributable to any incremental unit of a given pollutant remains constant across the simulation horizon. This computational convenience renders the EQI well suited for benchmarking control strategies but insensitive to the nonlinear cost escalation characteristic of actual regulatory regimes.

The piecewise-linear fine model of Stare et al. (2007), as adopted by Machado et al. (2020), introduces threshold-dependent nonlinearity through a dual-slope Heaviside structure. For each pollutant $j$, the effluent fine switches from a shallow slope $\Delta\alpha_j$ when $C_j^{\text{EFF}} \leq C_{L,j}$ to a steeper slope $\Delta\beta_j$ when $C_j^{\text{EFF}} > C_{L,j}$. This produces a discontinuous jump in marginal cost at the regulatory threshold, incentivizing compliance while still penalizing residual sub-limit pollution.

Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) embedded two contrasting penalty alternatives within a mixed-integer nonlinear programming model for macroscopic watershed management. Alternative 1 levies a proportional fine per gram of pollutant discharged above the environmental constraint, with the unit fine derived from the treatment cost required for compliance and scaled by an enforcement multiplier $\phi$. The marginal penalty is therefore constant per unit of excess mass but tuneable through $\phi$. Alternative 2 replaces this graduated charge with a fixed fine activated by a binary variable whenever any violation occurs, irrespective of its magnitude. The fixed fine is computed from the total treatment cost divided by the maximum permitted violation $M^p$ and scaled by $\phi^{\text{fixed}}$, introducing additional combinatorial complexity but stronger deterrence against any non-compliance.

The Catalan industrial tariff system, referenced in the context of the Manresa water resource recovery facility retrofit study (Machado et al., 2020), applies a tariff structure wherein the effective penalty coefficient escalates during peak discharge periods. Although this framework shares features with the piecewise-linear model, its distinguishing characteristic is the temporal modulation of the penalty rate through a peak coefficient tied to operational scheduling.

Within the domain of tradable discharge permits, Jin et al. (2014) formulated a two-stage dynamic game between a budget-constrained regulator and heterogeneous risk-neutral firms under costly enforcement. The penalty function $F(v_i) = F_0 + f(v_i)$ comprises both fixed and variable components, with the socially optimal strategy setting the marginal penalty $f'(0) = p/\pi$, where $p$ is the equilibrium permit price and $\pi$ is the monitoring probability. Extending this result, a dynamic regulatory strategy preannounces $f'(0) = Tp$ with $T > 1$, rendering enforcement robust to permit-price volatility. Schwartz and Stahn's theoretical antecedents, together with the computational experiments of Jin et al. (2014), demonstrate that market-clearing mechanisms endogenize the marginal cost entirely.

The Roberts and Spence three-part policy, discussed in the context of hybrid instrument design (Xenarios and Bithas, 2012), combines an initial permit allocation with a per-unit subsidy for emissions below the permit level and a per-unit charge for emissions above it. This banded structure bounds the effective marginal cost between the subsidy rate and the charge rate, hedging against uncertainty in abatement costs.

## Economic Equilibrium and the DR–Effluent Quality Tradeoff

Translating demand response (DR) aeration simulation outputs into a monetized optimization problem requires a formal equilibrium framework that jointly quantifies energy savings and effluent quality degradation in commensurate economic units. The methodological architecture rests on three interlinked components: (i) a time-weighted effluent quality index that aggregates pollution loads, (ii) an aeration energy cost model proportional to oxygen transfer intensity and reactor geometry, and (iii) a piecewise-linear fine function that converts effluent quality deviations into pollutant-specific monetary penalties. Together, these elements define a tradeoff surface on which the optimal DR operating point can be located through an equilibrium condition analogous to the permit-market clearing rule.

### Effluent Quality Aggregation via the EQI Integral

The Benchmark Simulation Model framework employs a dimensionless Effluent Quality Index (EQI) that integrates the weighted concentrations of multiple pollutants over a specified evaluation window. Following the conventions adopted in the BSM1 and BSM2 literature (Copp, 2002; Machado et al., 2020), the index is computed as:

$$EQI = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T \sum_j \omega_j \, C_j^{\text{eff}}(t) \, Q^{\text{eff}}(t) \, dt$$

where $\omega_j$ are pollutant-specific weighting factors calibrated to regional fine formulae, $C_j^{\text{eff}}(t)$ is the instantaneous effluent concentration of pollutant $j$, $Q^{\text{eff}}(t)$ is the effluent flow rate, and $T$ is the evaluation period. The weighting factors, historically calibrated to the Flanders regional tariff, assign relative monetary weights to total suspended solids, chemical oxygen demand, biological oxygen demand, total Kjeldahl nitrogen, and nitrate-nitrogen. Because the weights are fixed and the aggregation is linear, the marginal cost attributed to any incremental gram of a given pollutant remains constant within each simulation period, a property that is computationally convenient but insensitive to the nonlinear cost escalation real regulatory regimes impose once discharge limits are breached (Machado et al., 2020).

### BSM Aeration Energy Formula

Aeration constitutes the dominant electricity cost in activated sludge facilities, accounting for 45–60% of total energy consumption (Zohrabian et al., 2021). The BSM energy model expresses aeration energy $AE$ as a function of the volumetric oxygen transfer coefficient and reactor volume (Stare et al., 2007; Copp, 2002):

$$AE = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T \sum_i \frac{K_{La,i}(t) \cdot V_i}{V_{\text{ref}}} \, dt$$

where $K_{La,i}(t)$ is the oxygen transfer rate in reactor $i$, $V_i$ is the reactor volume, and $V_{\text{ref}}$ is a reference volume (typically 1333 m$^3$ in the standard BSM1 layout). This formulation ensures that any DR intervention that reduces $K_{La,i}$ during a curtailment window directly reduces $AE$, yielding quantifiable energy savings proportional to both the intensity and the duration of aeration reduction.

### Piecewise-Linear Fine Function

To recover the nonlinear regulatory cost structure suppressed by the linear EQI, the piecewise-linear effluent fine model introduced by Stare et al. (2007) decomposes the EQI back into pollutant-specific monetary penalties. For each pollutant $j$, the daily fine $EF_j$ is:

$$EF_j = \Delta\alpha_j \, C_j^{\text{eff}} \, Q^{\text{eff}} + H(C_j^{\text{eff}} - C_{L,j}) \left[ \Delta\beta_j (C_j^{\text{eff}} - C_{L,j}) \, Q^{\text{eff}} + b_{0,j} \, Q^{\text{eff}} \right]$$

where $\Delta\alpha_j$ is the slope of the fine curve below the discharge limit $C_{L,j}$, $\Delta\beta_j$ is the steeper slope above that limit, $b_{0,j}$ is a fixed surcharge for exceeding the limit, and $H(\cdot)$ is the Heaviside step function. This dual-slope Heaviside structure produces a discontinuous jump in marginal cost at the regulatory threshold, creating a strong economic incentive for the plant to remain compliant while still penalizing residual pollution below the limit (Machado et al., 2020). The total effluent fine is then $EF = \sum_j EF_j$, typically summing contributions from ammonium, total nitrogen, and phosphate.

### Equilibrium Condition: Marginal Abatement Cost Equals Shadow Price

The economic equilibrium governing the optimal DR operating point maps onto the classical permit-market clearing condition whereby the marginal abatement cost equals the permit price (Jin et al., 2014; Weitzman, 1974). In the DR context, this condition takes the following form: the marginal energy savings from an additional unit of aeration reduction must equal the marginal increase in effluent fines at the optimal operating point. Formally, if $S(\mathbf{x})$ denotes the energy savings function and $F(\mathbf{x})$ denotes the total effluent fine, both parameterized by the aeration reduction vector $\mathbf{x}$, optimality requires:

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial x_k} = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x_k} \quad \forall \, k$$

This condition mirrors the result of Jin et al. (2014), where the firm's marginal abatement cost $c_i'(a_i) = p$ at equilibrium, with $p$ the permit price. In the DR application, the shadow price of effluent quality — the implicit price the facility pays for each additional pollution unit discharged — plays the role of the permit price. As Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) demonstrated through sensitivity analysis of the enforcement multiplier $\phi$, deviations from this equilibrium either waste resources on excessive treatment or incur disproportionate fines.

### Three-Part Policy Banding

The shadow price of effluent quality can be bounded using a three-part policy structure analogous to the Roberts–Spence hybrid instrument described in the environmental economics literature (Xenarios and Bithas, 2012; Goulder and Parry, 2008). Under this design, a tax ceiling caps the maximum marginal cost the facility faces for additional discharge, while a subsidy floor establishes a minimum reward for emission reductions beyond the permit allocation. Within the interior band, the shadow price floats at the market-clearing level. This banding structure hedges against the regulator's uncertainty about true abatement costs and provides upper and lower bounds on the operating cost trajectory across a range of DR intensities. In the wastewater DR setting, the tax ceiling corresponds to the steeper fine slope $\Delta\beta_j$ triggered upon limit exceedance, while the subsidy floor can be interpreted as the baseline energy cost savings rate attainable under non-DR operation.

### Four-Dimensional Aeration Parameter Space

Constructing the response surfaces for both $S(\mathbf{x})$ and $F(\mathbf{x})$ requires systematic exploration of the aeration reduction design space. This space is spanned by four parameters: (1) the start time of the DR curtailment event within the diurnal cycle, (2) the duration of aeration reduction, (3) the intensity of $K_{La}$ reduction relative to the baseline setpoint, and (4) the frequency of curtailment events per evaluation period. Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) provides a space-filling experimental design that efficiently covers this four-dimensional domain, avoiding the combinatorial explosion of full-factorial designs while ensuring adequate coverage of parameter interactions. Each sample point defines a complete DR scenario that is propagated through the activated sludge process model to compute both the resulting $AE$ and $EQI$, from which $S$ and $F$ are derived. The resulting paired response surfaces — energy savings and effluent fines as functions of the four-dimensional aeration parameter vector — provide the empirical basis for locating the equilibrium operating point where marginal energy savings equal marginal fine increases, and for verifying that the solution lies within the bounds established by the three-part policy band.

## Cross-Cutting Findings and Implications for DR Simulation

Synthesizing the evidence from penalty framework design, pollutant parameterization, framework structural comparisons, and equilibrium analysis reveals a dominant quantitative result: nitrogen species carry marginal penalties an order of magnitude greater than organic parameters across every regulatory and benchmark costing framework examined. This asymmetry fundamentally shapes the feasibility envelope for demand response (DR) strategies that curtail aeration in activated sludge systems.

### Nitrogen Dominance in the Penalty Landscape

The piecewise-linear effluent fine model formalized by Stare et al. (2007) assigns below-limit discharge costs of $\Delta\alpha_{\mathrm{NH}} = 4$ €/kg for ammonium and $\Delta\alpha_{\mathrm{TN}} = 2.7$ €/kg for total nitrogen, values that already exceed those typically assigned to COD or BOD$_5$ in BSM-family evaluations. Upon limit exceedance, these costs escalate threefold to $\Delta\beta_{\mathrm{NH}} = 12$ €/kg and $\Delta\beta_{\mathrm{TN}} = 8.1$ €/kg (Stare et al., 2007). The Benchmark Simulation Model's Effluent Quality Index reinforces this hierarchy through weighting factors historically calibrated to the Flanders regional tariff, which assign total Kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN) and nitrate-nitrogen ($S_{\mathrm{NO}}$) weights that are 10–20× larger per unit mass than those for COD (Machado et al., 2020). Because the EQI aggregation is linear and the weights are fixed, the marginal cost attributed to each incremental gram of nitrogen remains constant within a simulation period yet already dominates the composite penalty (claim_2, claim_3). The practical consequence for DR simulation is unambiguous: even modest nitrification deteriorations induced by short aeration reductions will dominate the total penalty function, potentially overwhelming the energy cost savings that motivate the curtailment event.

This finding aligns with the control-strategy comparisons of Stare et al. (2007), who demonstrated that effluent fines decrease consistently with each successive advance in ammonia and nitrate feedback control, and that the optimal operating cost for each strategy "strongly depends on established effluent fines, i.e. selected parameters of $\Delta\alpha$, $\Delta\beta$ and $\beta_0$." When the Heaviside step contribution $\beta_0$ is removed and a flat per-kilogram fine is substituted, the incremental benefit of advanced controllers shrinks from approximately 12.6% to 6.2% (Stare et al., 2007). This sensitivity confirms that the nitrogen-weighted penalty structure, rather than aeration cost alone, is the binding constraint on DR headroom.

### The Heaviside Discontinuity and Solver Implications

The dual-slope fine function introduces a Heaviside discontinuity at the discharge limit concentration $C_{\mathrm{limit}}$, where the marginal penalty jumps from $\Delta\alpha_j$ to $\Delta\beta_j$ plus a fixed surcharge $\beta_{0,j}$ per unit volume (Stare et al., 2007). This discontinuity creates a non-smooth objective landscape that poses well-documented difficulties for gradient-based optimization solvers. In the equilibrium framework developed in the upstream analysis (claim_9, claim_12), the optimal DR operating point satisfies a condition analogous to permit-market clearing—marginal energy savings equal marginal fine increases—but locating this point computationally requires either derivative-free methods or smooth approximations of the step function. The Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) strategy adopted for the BSM1 simulation campaign (claim_21) sidesteps this difficulty by constructing empirical response surfaces over the four-dimensional aeration parameter space (start time, duration, $K_La$ reduction intensity, and event frequency), from which the equilibrium can be identified graphically or via interpolation rather than direct nonlinear programming.

### Variability Penalties and Intermittent Aeration

The Catalonia-based framework described by Machado et al. (2020) evaluates not only annual average and flow-weighted mean concentrations but also peak effluent concentrations of TN, TKN, $\mathrm{NH}_4^+$, and $\mathrm{NO}_3^-$ against European Community directive thresholds. Computing both the average and the peak introduces an implicit penalty on pollutant variability—a dimension absent from purely mean-based indices. For intermittent aeration strategies, which by definition induce cyclical dissolved oxygen transients and corresponding oscillations in nitrification rate (Stare et al., 2007; Zohrabian et al., 2021), this peak coefficient is particularly consequential. A DR curtailment event that preserves the time-averaged ammonia concentration but generates a transient spike above $C_{\mathrm{limit}}$ will trigger the steeper fine slope $\Delta\beta_j$ during the spike interval. The Catalonia peak criterion therefore penalizes exactly the temporal pattern that intermittent aeration produces, making it a more conservative—and arguably more realistic—test of DR feasibility than the standard BSM1 EQI.

### Endogeneity of the Correct Marginal Penalty

A central insight from the equilibrium analysis is that the marginal penalty governing the optimal DR intensity is endogenous rather than exogenously fixed (claim_11, claim_14). Drawing on the analogy to emissions trading established by Jin et al. (2014), the shadow price of effluent quality—the implicit cost the facility pays for each additional unit of discharge—emerges from the interaction between the facility's abatement cost function and the regulatory fine schedule. As Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) demonstrated through sensitivity analysis of the enforcement multiplier $\varphi$, deviations from this equilibrium either waste resources on excessive treatment or incur disproportionate fines (claim_15, claim_27). The three-part policy banding structure identified in the upstream synthesis—comprising a tax ceiling ($\Delta\beta_j$), a subsidy floor (baseline energy savings rate), and an interior floating shadow price (claim_16, claim_17)—provides operational bounds within which the endogenous penalty must lie. This endogeneity implies that fixed penalty weights, while computationally convenient, may systematically misrepresent the true marginal cost of DR-induced quality degradation, particularly near the discharge limit where the Heaviside jump amplifies pricing errors.

### Open Questions for the BSM1 Campaign

Several research gaps identified by Zohrabian et al. (2021) remain directly addressable through the planned LHS-sampled BSM1 simulation campaign. First, safe equipment shutdown durations during DR events are poorly characterized; pilot studies have tested shutdowns ranging from 15 to 120 minutes, with ammonia exceedances observed after repeated curtailments (Schäfer, 2020, cited in Zohrabian et al., 2021). Second, recovery times after DR events—the intervals required for nitrification capacity to return to steady-state performance—are not well quantified, yet they critically determine the feasible event frequency parameter in the four-dimensional design space (claim_20). Third, the sensitivity of effluent quality to process interruptions remains an active area of investigation; case studies have yielded contradictory results, with some facilities reporting negligible quality impacts and others observing increased turbidity after aeration curtailment (Aghajanzadeh, McKane, & Wray, 2015; Giberti et al., 2020, cited in Zohrabian et al., 2021). The operation and maintenance costs associated with frequent equipment cycling likewise remain uncertain (Zohrabian et al., 2021). By spanning the aeration parameter space with LHS samples and evaluating both energy savings and pollutant-specific effluent penalties at each sample point, the BSM1 campaign is positioned to generate the paired response surfaces (claim_29) needed to bound these unknowns and to identify the equilibrium operating envelope within which DR participation remains both economically rational and regulatorily compliant.
