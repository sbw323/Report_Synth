## Penalization Frameworks for Effluent Discharge

The regulation of wastewater effluent quality has given rise to a spectrum of penalization frameworks, ranging from simple weighted indices embedded in simulation benchmarks to endogenous market-clearing permit prices. This section synthesizes the principal families of frameworks encountered in the literature, organizing them along a complexity hierarchy that subsequent sections can exploit when comparing marginal-cost structures and optimization behavior.

### Dimensionless Weighted Pollution Units: The BSM1/BSM2 Approach

The Benchmark Simulation Models BSM1 and BSM2 employ a dimensionless effluent quality index that aggregates multiple pollutant concentrations into a single weighted sum expressed in pollution units per day. The weighting factors are calibrated to the Flanders regional fine formula, which assigns relative monetary weights to total suspended solids, chemical oxygen demand, biological oxygen demand, total Kjeldahl nitrogen, and nitrate-nitrogen. Because the weights are fixed and the aggregation is linear, the marginal cost attributed to any incremental gram of a given pollutant is constant within a simulation period. This renders the BSM framework computationally convenient for benchmarking control strategies but insensitive to the nonlinear cost escalation that real regulatory regimes impose once discharge limits are breached.

### Piecewise-Linear Monetary Fines: The Stare et al. Model

Stare et al. (2007) introduced a piecewise-linear effluent fine model that explicitly distinguishes between sub-limit and supra-limit discharge concentrations. For each pollutant $j$, the daily fine is computed as

$$EF_j = \Delta\alpha_j \, C_j^{\text{EFF}} + (\Delta\beta_j - \Delta\alpha_j)\,(C_j^{\text{EFF}} - C_{L,j})\,H(C_j^{\text{EFF}} - C_{L,j})$$

where $\Delta\alpha_j$ is the slope below the discharge limit $C_{L,j}$, $\Delta\beta_j$ is the steeper slope above that limit, and $H(\cdot)$ denotes the Heaviside step function (Machado et al., 2020; Stare et al., 2007). This dual-slope Heaviside structure produces a discontinuous jump in marginal cost at the regulatory threshold, creating a strong economic incentive for the plant to remain compliant while still penalizing residual pollution below the limit. The model has been deployed extensively in water resource recovery facility optimization studies, including retrofitting comparisons where effluent fines for ammonium, total nitrogen, and phosphate are summed alongside energy and sludge costs (Machado et al., 2020).

### Proportional and Fixed Alternatives: Garibay-Rodriguez et al.

Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) formulated two contrasting penalty alternatives within a mixed-integer nonlinear programming (MINLP) model for macroscopic watershed management. Under Alternative 1, a proportional fine $F^d$ (or $F^i$ for industrial sources) is charged per gram of pollutant discharged above the environmental constraint. The fine magnitude is derived from the unit treatment cost required to achieve compliance, scaled by an enforcement multiplier $\phi$ such that $F^d = \phi \cdot CPG^d$, where $CPG^d$ is the cost per gram of pollution reduction through technology alone (Garibay-Rodriguez et al., 2017). This proportional structure yields a marginal penalty that is constant per unit of excess mass but tuneable through $\phi$; sensitivity analysis demonstrates that the system's economic–environmental trade-off is highly responsive to $\phi$, with values near 0.1 representing an inflection point beyond which additional penalty increases raise costs without further reducing discharge concentrations (Garibay-Rodriguez et al., 2017).

Alternative 2 replaces the proportional charge with a fixed fine $CFF^d$ activated by a binary variable $w^{p}_{r,d,l,t}$ whenever the discharge limit is violated, irrespective of the magnitude of transgression. The fixed fine is derived from the total treatment cost divided by the maximum permitted violation $M^p$ and scaled by $\phi^{\text{fixed}}$. Although the fixed-fine alternative adds combinatorial complexity through additional binary variables, it offers stronger environmental protection in the studied cases, since it discourages any violation rather than merely pricing marginal exceedances (Garibay-Rodriguez et al., 2017).

### The Catalonia Industrial Tariff

Regional tariff systems, such as the Catalonia industrial wastewater tariff, incorporate peak-flow coefficients and volume saturation mechanisms that produce nonlinear effective penalties. A peak coefficient multiplies the base tariff rate during periods of hydraulic overload, while a saturation function caps the volumetric charge at high discharge rates, reflecting diminishing marginal infrastructure cost beyond design capacity. These features embed an implicit penalty for both concentration and flow exceedances within the tariff structure itself, rather than through an external fine.

### Equilibrium Permit Market Frameworks

At the most complex end of the hierarchy lie frameworks in which the marginal cost of pollution is determined endogenously through market equilibrium rather than being set administratively. Schwartz and Stahn developed theoretical conditions under which tradable discharge permits achieve cost-effective pollution control in water systems, leveraging the allocative efficiency of price-clearing markets. Jin et al. (2014) extended this line of inquiry by studying a two-stage dynamic game between a regulator and heterogeneous risk-neutral firms under costly enforcement. Their model specifies a penalty function $F(v_i) = F_0 + f(v_i)$ with both fixed ($F_0$) and variable components, and demonstrates that the socially optimal regulatory strategy is to induce full compliance by setting the marginal penalty equal to the permit price divided by the monitoring probability, i.e., $f'(0) = p/\pi$ (Jin et al., 2014). A key insight is that integrating the permit market price directly into the penalty shape — so that $f'(0) = Tp$ with $T > 1$ — endogenizes the penalty and renders the enforcement strategy robust to permit-price volatility.

Roberts and Spence proposed a three-part policy combining an initial permit allocation, a per-unit subsidy for emissions below the permit level, and a per-unit charge for emissions above it. This hybrid instrument bounds the effective marginal cost between the subsidy rate and the charge rate, hedging against the regulator's uncertainty about abatement costs. Xenarios and Bithas (2012) corroborate that hybrid instruments combining standards with taxes or permits frequently outperform any single tool, a finding consistent with the broader environmental economics literature on second-best instrument choice.

### Complexity Hierarchy

The frameworks described above can be ordered along a continuum of increasing endogeneity and nonlinearity. At one extreme, the BSM weighted sum assigns fixed, exogenous weights that yield constant marginal penalties. The Stare et al. piecewise model introduces a threshold nonlinearity through the Heaviside function, producing two distinct marginal-cost regimes. The Garibay-Rodriguez proportional and fixed alternatives embed the penalty within an optimization model, making the realized penalty a decision variable modulated by $\phi$ or by binary activation. The Catalonia tariff further complicates matters with volume-dependent saturation. Finally, the permit-market frameworks of Jin et al. (2014) and the Roberts–Spence hybrid render the marginal cost fully endogenous, determined by market clearing and regulatory design simultaneously. This hierarchy — from fixed weighted sums through piecewise functions to endogenous equilibrium prices — provides the organizational scaffold for the comparative analyses that follow.
