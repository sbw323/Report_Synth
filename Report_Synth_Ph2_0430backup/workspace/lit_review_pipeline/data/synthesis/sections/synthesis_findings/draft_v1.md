## Cross-Cutting Findings and Implications for DR Simulation

Synthesizing the evidence from penalty framework design, pollutant parameterization, framework structural comparisons, and equilibrium analysis reveals a dominant quantitative result: nitrogen species carry marginal penalties an order of magnitude greater than organic parameters across every regulatory and benchmark costing framework examined. This asymmetry fundamentally shapes the feasibility envelope for demand response (DR) strategies that curtail aeration in activated sludge systems.

### Nitrogen Dominance in the Penalty Landscape

The piecewise-linear effluent fine model formalized by Stare et al. (2007) assigns below-limit discharge costs of $\Delta\alpha_{\mathrm{NH}} = 4$ €/kg for ammonium and $\Delta\alpha_{\mathrm{TN}} = 2.7$ €/kg for total nitrogen, values that already exceed those typically assigned to COD or BOD$_5$ in BSM-family evaluations. Upon limit exceedance, these costs escalate threefold to $\Delta\beta_{\mathrm{NH}} = 12$ €/kg and $\Delta\beta_{\mathrm{TN}} = 8.1$ €/kg (Stare et al., 2007). The Benchmark Simulation Model's Effluent Quality Index reinforces this hierarchy through weighting factors historically calibrated to the Flanders regional tariff, which assign total Kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN) and nitrate-nitrogen ($S_{\mathrm{NO}}$) weights that are 10–20× larger per unit mass than those for COD (Machado et al., 2020). Because the EQI aggregation is linear and the weights are fixed, the marginal cost attributed to each incremental gram of nitrogen remains constant within a simulation period yet already dominates the composite penalty (claim_2, claim_3). The practical consequence for DR simulation is unambiguous: even modest nitrification deteriorations induced by short aeration reductions will dominate the total penalty function, potentially overwhelming the energy cost savings that motivate the curtailment event.

This finding aligns with the control-strategy comparisons of Stare et al. (2007), who demonstrated that effluent fines decrease consistently with each successive advance in ammonia and nitrate feedback control, and that the optimal operating cost for each strategy "strongly depends on established effluent fines, i.e. selected parameters of $\Delta\alpha$, $\Delta\beta$ and $\beta_0$." When the Heaviside step contribution $\beta_0$ is removed and a flat per-kilogram fine is substituted, the incremental benefit of advanced controllers shrinks from approximately 12.6% to 6.2% (Stare et al., 2007). This sensitivity confirms that the nitrogen-weighted penalty structure, rather than aeration cost alone, is the binding constraint on DR headroom.

### The Heaviside Discontinuity and Solver Implications

The dual-slope fine function introduces a Heaviside discontinuity at the discharge limit concentration $C_{\mathrm{limit}}$, where the marginal penalty jumps from $\Delta\alpha_j$ to $\Delta\beta_j$ plus a fixed surcharge $\beta_{0,j}$ per unit volume (Stare et al., 2007). This discontinuity creates a non-smooth objective landscape that poses well-documented difficulties for gradient-based optimization solvers. In the equilibrium framework developed in the upstream analysis (claim_9, claim_12), the optimal DR operating point satisfies a condition analogous to permit-market clearing—marginal energy savings equal marginal fine increases—but locating this point computationally requires either derivative-free methods or smooth approximations of the step function. The Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) strategy adopted for the BSM1 simulation campaign (claim_21) sidesteps this difficulty by constructing empirical response surfaces over the four-dimensional aeration parameter space (start time, duration, $K_La$ reduction intensity, and event frequency), from which the equilibrium can be identified graphically or via interpolation rather than direct nonlinear programming.

### Variability Penalties and Intermittent Aeration

The Catalonia-based framework described by Machado et al. (2020) evaluates not only annual average and flow-weighted mean concentrations but also peak effluent concentrations of TN, TKN, $\mathrm{NH}_4^+$, and $\mathrm{NO}_3^-$ against European Community directive thresholds. Computing both the average and the peak introduces an implicit penalty on pollutant variability—a dimension absent from purely mean-based indices. For intermittent aeration strategies, which by definition induce cyclical dissolved oxygen transients and corresponding oscillations in nitrification rate (Stare et al., 2007; Zohrabian et al., 2021), this peak coefficient is particularly consequential. A DR curtailment event that preserves the time-averaged ammonia concentration but generates a transient spike above $C_{\mathrm{limit}}$ will trigger the steeper fine slope $\Delta\beta_j$ during the spike interval. The Catalonia peak criterion therefore penalizes exactly the temporal pattern that intermittent aeration produces, making it a more conservative—and arguably more realistic—test of DR feasibility than the standard BSM1 EQI.

### Endogeneity of the Correct Marginal Penalty

A central insight from the equilibrium analysis is that the marginal penalty governing the optimal DR intensity is endogenous rather than exogenously fixed (claim_11, claim_14). Drawing on the analogy to emissions trading established by Jin et al. (2014), the shadow price of effluent quality—the implicit cost the facility pays for each additional unit of discharge—emerges from the interaction between the facility's abatement cost function and the regulatory fine schedule. As Garibay-Rodriguez et al. (2017) demonstrated through sensitivity analysis of the enforcement multiplier $\varphi$, deviations from this equilibrium either waste resources on excessive treatment or incur disproportionate fines (claim_15, claim_27). The three-part policy banding structure identified in the upstream synthesis—comprising a tax ceiling ($\Delta\beta_j$), a subsidy floor (baseline energy savings rate), and an interior floating shadow price (claim_16, claim_17)—provides operational bounds within which the endogenous penalty must lie. This endogeneity implies that fixed penalty weights, while computationally convenient, may systematically misrepresent the true marginal cost of DR-induced quality degradation, particularly near the discharge limit where the Heaviside jump amplifies pricing errors.

### Open Questions for the BSM1 Campaign

Several research gaps identified by Zohrabian et al. (2021) remain directly addressable through the planned LHS-sampled BSM1 simulation campaign. First, safe equipment shutdown durations during DR events are poorly characterized; pilot studies have tested shutdowns ranging from 15 to 120 minutes, with ammonia exceedances observed after repeated curtailments (Schäfer, 2020, cited in Zohrabian et al., 2021). Second, recovery times after DR events—the intervals required for nitrification capacity to return to steady-state performance—are not well quantified, yet they critically determine the feasible event frequency parameter in the four-dimensional design space (claim_20). Third, the sensitivity of effluent quality to process interruptions remains an active area of investigation; case studies have yielded contradictory results, with some facilities reporting negligible quality impacts and others observing increased turbidity after aeration curtailment (Aghajanzadeh, McKane, & Wray, 2015; Giberti et al., 2020, cited in Zohrabian et al., 2021). The operation and maintenance costs associated with frequent equipment cycling likewise remain uncertain (Zohrabian et al., 2021). By spanning the aeration parameter space with LHS samples and evaluating both energy savings and pollutant-specific effluent penalties at each sample point, the BSM1 campaign is positioned to generate the paired response surfaces (claim_29) needed to bound these unknowns and to identify the equilibrium operating envelope within which DR participation remains both economically rational and regulatorily compliant.
